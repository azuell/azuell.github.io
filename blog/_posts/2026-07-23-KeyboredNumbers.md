The [Keybored](https://melbourne.sciencegallery.com/distraction-exhibits/keybored) was an artwork I made for the Science Gallery Melbourne. After 9 months of operation, **22995**{:.hl-green} messages were sent by the public using the keyboard to the Keybored, and **169961**{: .hl-green} message dings were received on the keyphones.
<br>
<br>
<p class="quote"><strong>This is an analysis of those messages.</strong></p>
<figure class="img-large">
  <img src="/blog/assets/KeyboredNumbers/keyboard_layout.png" alt="Keyboard in the Keybored colour way, with black and white keys to mimic a piano">
  <figcaption>Keyboard in the Keybored colour way, with black and white keys to mimic a piano octave</figcaption>
</figure>
<br>
<h2>Time</h2>
<br>
Let's start by taking a look at the spread of messages over time. The exhibition opened on **Saturday 26th July 2025**{: .hl-green} and ran until **Saturday 2nd May 2026**{: .hl-green}, with a break over the summer from **Sunday 21st December 2025**{: .hl-green} to **Wednesday 18th February 2026**{: .hl-green}. 
<br>
<br>
<figure class="img-large">
  <img src="/blog/assets/KeyboredNumbers/messages_per_day_labelled.png" alt="Bar chart titled 'Messages sent per day' spanning August 2025 to May 2026, with annotations marking 'Exhibition starts', the 'Busiest day', 'Break starts', 'Break ends', and 'Exhibition ends'">
  <figcaption>Graph of messages sent to the Keybored over the duration of the exhibition</figcaption>
</figure>
The most busy day for messaging was **Friday 29th August 2025**{: .hl-yellow} with a whopping **4375**{: .hl-yellow} messages. This was the day of the Friday Night Social event hosted by Now or Never, with **301**{: .hl-yellow} messages after 5pm during the event. To put this in perspective, over the 172 days that had any activity on the Keybored, an average of only **133**{: .hl-green} messages were sent each day.
<br>
<br>
<figure class="img-large">
  <img src="/blog/assets/KeyboredNumbers/weekday_message_distribution.png" alt="Bar chart of total messages by day of the week: Monday 2946, Tuesday 8737, Wednesday 40519, Thursday 38688, Friday 39591, Saturday 60632, Sunday 2009">
  <figcaption>Graph of messages sent to the Keyboard totalled over day of the week</figcaption>
</figure>
It's worth noting the Science Gallery was only open to the public **Wednesday**{: .hl-pink} to **Saturday**{: .hl-blue}, so any messages from other days of the week were limited to school groups, private tours and special events. I'm curious to know whether this graph (and the one before it) mirrors the visitation patterns seen for the duration of the exhibition. Unsurprisingly, the busiest day of week was **Saturday**{: .hl-blue} by a wide margin, roughly 1.5x the next busiest day of the week.  
<br>
<br>
<figure class="img-large">
  <img src="/blog/assets/KeyboredNumbers/messages_time_of_day.png" alt="Horizontal bar chart of messages sent during gallery opening hours: 11am 3357, 12pm 3798, 1pm 3949, 2pm 3935, 3pm 3418, 4pm 2741">
  <figcaption>Graph of messages sent to the Keyboard by time of day sent</figcaption>
</figure>
Nothing too exciting hiding in the hour-by-hour daily breakdown of message numbers. Messages peak at **1pm**{: .hl-pink} in the middle of the day, and follow an expected slow ramp up in the morning and ramp down in the afternoon. Typically the morning is busier than the afternoon.
<br>
<br>
<h2>Content</h2>
<br>
Now we know *when* messages were sent, *what* did they say? The most popular message sent to the Keybored, by an absolute landslide, was a simple **"hello"**{: .hl-yellow}, sent **795**{: .hl-yellow} times.
<figure class="img-large">
  <img src="/blog/assets/KeyboredNumbers/top_10_messages.png" alt="Leaderboard of the 10 most frequently sent messages: hello (795), I love you (52), how are you (51), hello world (50), love (40), lol (32), hey (32), bye (31), what (29), penis (25)">
  <figcaption>Leaderboard of the top 10 messages sent to the Keybored</figcaption>
</figure>
The longest message was **1055**{: .hl-green} characters, and was a random key smash. The average message was just over **20**{: .hl-pink} characters in length. There were many more interesting and insightful messages sent to the Keybored beyond one word greetings, such as **"ARE YOU TRACKING WHAT I TYPE"**{: .hl-yellow} and **"i wonder if they’re tracking down what peoiple write?"**{: .hl-red} :) 
<br>
<br>
<h2>Letters</h2>
<br>
Easier than analysing message content and sentiment, we can instead take a look at the messages sent and break it down by letter or character. The most frequently appearing letter was **H**{: .hl-blue}, likely due to the sheer number of "hello", "hey" and "how are you"s. **H**{: .hl-blue} is also nice and centred physically on a keyboard, making it a prime candidate for keyboard smashing.
<figure class="img-large">
  <img src="/blog/assets/KeyboredNumbers/letter_freq.png" alt="Bar chart of letter frequency in Keybored messages, with H the most common at 7.1%, followed by E, A, O and I">
  <figcaption>Graph of letter frequency in Keyboard messages</figcaption>
</figure>
<figure class="img-large">
  <img src="/blog/assets/KeyboredNumbers/letter_freq_eng_colour.png" alt="Bar chart of standard English letter frequency, topped by E at 12.7%, followed by T, A, O and I">
  <figcaption>Graph of letter frequency in the English language</figcaption>
</figure>
We can compare the frequency of letter usage on the Keybored against the expected frequency of popularity of letters in English. Across the board we can see a much flatter distribution on the Keybored, likely due to the prevalence of random inputs - even the least popular letters get a bit more action. The middling letters like **G**{: .hl-yellow} and **F**{: .hl-pink} have also risen in frequency, likely due to their prime location in the physical centre of the keyboard.
<br>
<br>
<figure class="img-large">
  <img src="/blog/assets/KeyboredNumbers/keyboard_heatmap.png" alt="Heatmap of the keyboard with each key labelled by message count and colour-coded from yellow (lowest) to red (highest); H is hottest at 24164, followed by E (22876), A (20547) and O (19010)">
  <figcaption>Heatmap of letter popularity - Red highest</figcaption>
</figure>
Mapping the letters back onto the keyboard, we can see the hot characters in red. Another pattern starts to emerge - black keys towards the middle like **E**{: .hl-red} & **D**{: .hl-red} as well as **Y**{: .hl-red} & **H**{: .hl-red} see a boost in usage. Interestingly, the numbers were overall not as popular as I anticipated, and no one number sticks out as more popular. The even spread across the numbers also works against my theory about black keys - if they were more popular, we should expect to see some bump to 1, 3, 6, 8 or 0, but we do not. Comparing numbers to letters (and punctuation) does feel a little unfair though, so I don't think it holds too much weight. Perhaps if we saw the opposite effect, or even just any number standing out more than any others I would be a bit more concerned.
<br>
<br>
Each keyboard key was part of a group representing a note on an octave. These groups were unevenly sized and not intended to create a balanced distribution, but rather creating the visual appearance of piano keys across a mechanical keyboard.
<figure class="img-large">
  <img src="/blog/assets/KeyboredNumbers/keyboard_key_groups.png" alt="Diagram of the keyboard with keys colour-coded into 12 unevenly sized key phone groups, each corresponding to a repeating piano note">
  <figcaption>Keyboard keys grouped by their piano key</figcaption>
</figure>
Unsurprisingly, groups with more letters in them sent more messages to their key phone. The black key
closest to the centre, **Key Phone 07**{: .hl-green} (6, Y, H) bucks this trend. It sees more usage than **Key Phone 05**{: .hl-yellow} (4, R, F, C) and **Key Phone 10**{: .hl-yellow} (9, O, L, <, >), despite having fewer characters. Perhaps it is being carried by the power of the most popular **H**{: .hl-green}, as well as being a centrally located black set of characters.
<figure class="img-large">
  <img src="/blog/assets/KeyboredNumbers/messages_per_key.png" alt="Bar chart of messages sent to each key phone: Key Phone 01 - 2142, Key Phone 02 - 11772, Key Phone 03 - 18803, Key Phone 04 - 15496, Key Phone 05 - 17496, Key Phone 06 - 21825, Key Phone 07 - 18467, Key Phone 08 - 23671, Key Phone 09 - 15081, Key Phone 10 - 18221, Key Phone 11 - 4557, Key Phone 12 - 2431">
  <figcaption>Graph of messages sent to each key</figcaption>
</figure>
Seeing **Key Phone 01**{: .hl-blue} (Z) with its lone character, unpopular on a good day let alone carrying a whole key on its back, and **Key Phone 12**{: .hl-green} (-, {, ", ?) with only unpopular punctuation coming in last should also come as no surprise. 
**Key Phone 02**{: .hl-green} (1, Q, A) gets absolutely carried by the letter **A**{: .hl-green} in it, seeing as it nearly triples in number compared with its similar black key trio on the other side of the keyboard, **Key Phone 11**{: .hl-blue} (0, P, :).
